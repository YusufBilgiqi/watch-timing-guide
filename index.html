02 ‚Äî The Core Concept

Time Is Counted in Vibrations

Here's the fundamental insight: a mechanical watch doesn't use a clock or digital timer. It literally counts vibrations to measure time passing.

Example ‚Äî 18,000 VPH watch:

18,000 vibrations √∑ 3,600 seconds = 5 vibrations/second

So each vibration represents exactly 1/5th of a second. The watch's gears are designed to count exactly 18,000 vibrations as "one hour."

Now here's the critical part: if the balance wheel vibrates faster or slower than designed, the watch gains or loses time. It's like a factory conveyor belt ‚Äî if the belt speeds up, boxes come out faster than expected.

03 ‚Äî The Controls

What Controls the Vibration Rate?

Surprisingly few things determine how fast or slow the balance wheel vibrates. They all come down to the properties of the hairspring and balance wheel.

üåÄ The Hairspring

üìè Length of the Hairspring
Shorter spring ‚Üí vibrates faster ‚Üí watch gains time

Think of a guitar string. A short string vibrates at a high pitch (fast). A long string vibrates at a low pitch (slow). Same idea here ‚Äî a shorter hairspring means more vibrations per second.
üí™ Stiffness of the Hairspring
Stiffer spring ‚Üí vibrates faster ‚Üí watch gains time

Like a stiff vs. floppy diving board. A stiff board snaps back fast (high frequency). A floppy one wobbles slowly. A stiffer hairspring returns the balance wheel to center faster.

üé∏ The Guitar String Analogy

‚öñÔ∏è The Balance Wheel

‚öñÔ∏è Mass / Weight
Lighter wheel ‚Üí vibrates faster ‚Üí watch gains time

Like spinning a light vs. heavy top. The light one spins easily and quickly. A heavier balance wheel has more inertia ‚Äî harder to get going, harder to stop ‚Äî so it vibrates more slowly.
üî© Mass Distribution (Timing Screws)
Mass toward center ‚Üí faster. Mass at rim ‚Üí slower.

Like a figure skater: arms in = spins fast. Arms out = spins slow. Timing screws at the rim of the balance wheel can be moved in or out to fine-tune the rate.

04 ‚Äî Fine Tuning

The Regulator: How a Watchmaker Adjusts the Rate

The regulator is a small lever on the movement with two tiny pins that grip the hairspring. By sliding it, the watchmaker effectively changes the active length of the hairspring ‚Äî the part that's actually doing the work.

06 ‚Äî Troubleshooting

What Can Cause the Rate to Change?

Now that we understand how the vibration rate controls timekeeping, here are the real-world culprits that mess with that rate:

üß≤
Gains Time

Magnetism

If the hairspring gets magnetized, its coils can stick together ‚Äî effectively shortening the spring and causing it to vibrate faster. This is very common (phones, speakers, bag clasps). The fix: demagnetize the watch.

üåÄ
Gains Time

Hairspring Touching the Bridge

If the hairspring isn't perfectly centered, a coil can rub against the bridge above it. This acts like the regulator moving ‚Äî shortening the effective spring length and speeding things up.

üåä
Loses Time

Hairspring Out of Flat (Deformed)

If the hairspring is bent or warped (not lying flat), the coils don't work uniformly. The spring becomes effectively "softer" in some positions, slowing the vibration rate down.

üå°Ô∏è
Can Go Either Way

Temperature Changes

Heat softens the hairspring (slower vibration = loses time). Cold stiffens it (faster vibration = gains time). Most modern alloys like Nivarox are engineered to minimize this effect.

üîß
Watchmaker Controlled

Regulator Position

If the regulator pins drift or are set incorrectly, the active length of the hairspring changes ‚Äî directly shifting the rate up or down. This is the primary tuning tool a watchmaker uses.

üî©
Balance Wheel Issue

Timing Screws (if present)

On watches with adjustable timing screws at the rim of the balance wheel, screws turned in = faster, turned out = slower. These are another way to fine-tune the mass distribution.

07 ‚Äî Common Misconception

The Amplitude Myth

Many people assume that if a watch has low amplitude (the balance wheel isn't swinging very far), that's why it's losing time. This is a common misunderstanding.

The truth: Amplitude and rate are separate things. A watch with low amplitude (say, 200¬∞) can still be adjusted to run accurately ‚Äî because the number of vibrations per second can be the same regardless of how far the wheel swings. Amplitude matters for other reasons (power reserve, shock protection) but it doesn't directly determine rate gain or loss.

What does cause rate issues is a change in the vibration frequency ‚Äî whether caused by a magnetized spring, a deformed spring, incorrect regulator position, or anything else that alters how many times per second the balance completes a vibration.

The One Sentence to Remember

"It is the vibration rate ‚Äî not amplitude ‚Äî that controls whether a watch gains or loses time."

Everything else (hairspring length, stiffness, balance mass, temperature, magnetism) only matters because it ultimately changes the vibration rate. Fix the rate, fix the watch.

08 ‚Äî Bonus: Factory Matching

Why Not All Watches Are Created Equal

ETA (one of the world's biggest movement makers) can't manufacture every balance wheel and hairspring to be perfectly identical. So they do something clever: they test each component and categorize them into 20 groups based on their properties, then match the best-paired groups together.

üìä Balance Wheels ‚Üí 20 Groups
Each wheel is tested for its moment of inertia ‚Äî how much it resists rotation. Lighter or differently shaped wheels go into different groups.
üåÄ Hairsprings ‚Üí 20 Groups
Each hairspring is tested for its couple value ‚Äî the torque it can produce. Stiffer or longer springs go into different groups.

The best-matched pairs hit 18,000 VPH almost perfectly ‚Äî these go into Chronometer grade movements. The less well-matched pairs still work fine but need more regulator adjustment ‚Äî these become Standard grade. This is why replacing just the hairspring (without replacing the balance wheel too) can throw off the rate: you're mismatching components that were designed to be used together.

üí° Always replace the balance wheel and hairspring as a matched set (a "balance complete"). Mixing grades is like putting a Formula 1 engine in a different car's transmission ‚Äî the individual parts might work, but they're not tuned to work together.
